Project Overview:

This project investigates the steady-state thermal behavior of a CPU heat sink using numerical simulation techniques. The primary objective is to optimize the fin length to ensure efficient heat dissipation while maintaining a maximum temperature below 98°C. Finite Element Method (FEM) in MATLAB and ANSYS simulations were utilized to analyze the temperature distribution and validate the optimization results.

Methodologies:

  • Constructed the geometry of the heat sink in **Design Modeller ANSYS** and divided the structure into three regions: **Aluminum, Silicon, and FR-4**.
  • Implemented **Steady-State Thermal Solver** to simulate the temperature distribution under given boundary conditions.
  • Developed a **Finite Element Model (FEM)** in MATLAB using **Galerkin FEM with linear triangular elements** to solve the thermal equation.
  • Validated the numerical results against **ANSYS simulations** to ensure accuracy.
  • Conducted fin length optimization by iteratively adjusting the aluminum fin size to achieve a maximum temperature below 98°C.

Findings:

  • Using a **tetrahedral mesh** with **0.001m** resolution provided an optimal balance between accuracy and computational cost.
  • Temperature distribution results showed that reducing mesh size improved numerical accuracy.
  • The initial maximum temperature was **229.06°C**, which required fin length optimization to reduce it.
  • Through iterative optimization, an aluminum fin length of **42mm** was determined as the optimal solution, achieving a final maximum temperature of **96.56°C**.
  • The numerical model showed a **3.86% deviation** from the ANSYS simulation results, attributed to numerical assumptions.

Project Overview:

This project investigates the **transient heat transfer** behavior of a **heated pan**, modeled using **numerical methods** in MATLAB and validated against **ANSYS Transient Thermal Solver**. A **2D finite element method (FEM) solver** was developed to simulate heat conduction over time, implementing an **implicit backward-time scheme** for stability. The study analyzes temperature distribution, evaluates numerical accuracy, and compares MATLAB results against commercial software to highlight the effectiveness of custom solvers.

Methodology:

  • Developed a 2D transient heat conduction solver using the Galerkin Finite Element Method (FEM) in MATLAB.
  • Implemented an implicit backward-time discretization scheme for numerical stability.
  • Validated solver accuracy through grid refinement and time-step convergence analysis.
  • Simulated heat conduction using ANSYS Transient Thermal Solver with appropriate boundary conditions.
  • Compared MATLAB vs. ANSYS results to assess solver efficiency and error sources.

Key Findings:

  • Solver Performance & Accuracy:
    • Numerical methods demonstrated strong convergence with optimized mesh and time steps.
    • The implicit scheme improved stability and computational efficiency.
  • Heat Transfer Behavior:
    • Heat conducted radially, with rapid temperature increase before stabilization.
    • Insulation significantly reduced heat transfer to the handle, maintaining lower temperatures.
  • MATLAB vs. ANSYS Comparison:
    • MATLAB results underpredicted temperatures due to 2D simplifications and convection modeling differences.
    • Both methods followed expected heat transfer trends, confirming model validity.
  • Future Improvements:
    • Extend to 3D FEM modeling for improved accuracy.
    • Implement higher-order elements to reduce numerical diffusion.
    • Enhance convection modeling for more realistic heat transfer representation.
